# Elasticity of Substitution: Methodology, Findings, and Robustness

## Overview

This document describes the empirical analysis testing whether AI capital and specification labor are complements (σ < 1) or substitutes (σ > 1). It extends the core specification-labor findings (see `specification_labor_findings.md`) by estimating bounds on the elasticity of substitution.

**Bottom line:** Three independent approaches — aggregate trends, cross-sectional wage structure, and CPS microdata — all produce results directionally consistent with σ < 1. The strongest result is a cross-sectional wage interaction that is statistically significant when pooled across years (p = 0.002 parametric) and specific to the management-derived specification axis (placebo test: 100th percentile of random task axes). However, cluster-robust inference widens confidence intervals to include σ = 1 (bootstrap 95% CI for the pooled interaction: [-0.12, +0.75]). The honest assessment is σ ∈ [0.3, 1.5] with the weight of evidence below 1 — consistent with Oberfield & Raval's (2021, Econometrica) finding of σ ≈ 0.5–0.7 for US manufacturing.

## 1. Framework

### Production structure

We decompose the economy's production function into three inputs:

**Y = F(K, L_spec, L_exec)**

- **K** = AI/automation capital
- **L_spec** = specification labor (directing, allocating, staffing, deciding what to produce)
- **L_exec** = execution labor (implementing, processing, producing)

Under CES, the elasticity of substitution σ(K, L_spec) governs how AI capital and specification labor interact:

| σ | Interpretation | Prediction |
|---|----------------|------------|
| σ < 1 | Complements | More AI → *higher* demand for spec labor; spec wage premium rises with AI exposure |
| σ = 1 | Cobb-Douglas | AI doesn't change relative demand; factor shares constant |
| σ > 1 | Substitutes | More AI → *lower* demand for spec labor; AI replaces specification |

### Key testable predictions

Under σ < 1:
1. **Cross-sectional wages:** Occupations with higher AI exposure should pay a *larger* spec premium (β(spec × AIOE) > 0 in wage regression)
2. **Aggregate trends:** Relative spec employment can rise substantially with only modest relative wage decline (large demand shift absorbs quantity change)
3. **Microdata:** Workers in spec-intensive roles at AI-adopting firms should earn disproportionately more

## 3. Track A: Aggregate Trends (Katz-Murphy Framework)

**Script:** `elasticity_bounds.py`, section A1–A4

### Approach

The Katz-Murphy (1992) framework relates relative wages and relative employment under CES:

```
ln(w_spec/w_exec) = α + γ·t − (1/σ)·ln(L_spec/L_exec) + ε
```

The coefficient on ln(employment ratio) = −1/σ.

### Data

From `spec_exec_ratios_over_time.csv` (generated by `elasticity_estimation.py`):

| Year | Spec Emp (M) | Exec Emp (M) | Emp Ratio | Spec Wage | Exec Wage | Wage Ratio |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 2005 | 30.2 | 32.2 | 0.94 | $51,045 | $24,623 | 2.07 |
| 2009 | 32.1 | 30.1 | 1.07 | $57,684 | $27,635 | 2.09 |
| 2014 | 38.6 | 31.9 | 1.21 | $63,547 | $29,537 | 2.15 |
| 2019 | 41.3 | 37.3 | 1.11 | $69,770 | $33,248 | 2.10 |
| 2024 | 50.1 | 37.0 | 1.35 | $83,879 | $42,821 | 1.96 |

### Key finding

- **Δln(L_spec/L_exec) = +0.37** (44% relative employment increase toward spec)
- **Δln(w_spec/w_exec) = −0.06** (5% relative wage decline)
- Massive quantity shift with tiny price response → a large demand shift is absorbing most of the employment reallocation
- Under the D ≥ 0 constraint (AI shifts demand toward spec labor): **σ ≤ 6.5**
- With only 5 time points, the Katz-Murphy regression is uninformative for point estimation (β ≈ 0, SE huge)

### Figures

- **`elast_katz_murphy.png`** — Three panels: relative employment trend, relative wage trend, implied demand shift D(t) for candidate σ values. All σ ∈ [0.3, 2.0] give plausible smooth demand paths.

### Assessment

The aggregate trends are **consistent with** σ < 1 but do not pin it down — 5 time points cannot distinguish σ = 0.5 from σ = 1.5. The aggregate evidence is most useful for establishing that a large demand shift toward specification labor occurred.

## 4. Track A (continued): Cross-Sectional Wage Interaction

**Script:** `elasticity_estimation.py` Phase 3; `elasticity_bounds.py` section A2, A4

### Approach

For each year, regress log occupation wages on spec_residual, AIOE, their interaction, and PC1 (complexity), weighted by employment:

```
ln(wage_j) = α + β₁·spec_residual_j + β₂·AIOE_j + β₃·(spec × AIOE)_j + γ·PC1_j + ε_j
```

Under CES: **β₃ > 0 implies σ < 1** (AI exposure amplifies the specification wage premium).

### Results

| Year | β₃ (spec × AIOE) | SE | p-value | 95% CI |
|------|-------------------|-----|---------|--------|
| 2005 | +0.362 | 0.244 | 0.138 | [−0.12, +0.84] |
| 2009 | +0.357 | 0.237 | 0.132 | [−0.11, +0.82] |
| 2014 | +0.328 | 0.229 | 0.152 | [−0.12, +0.78] |
| 2019 | +0.303 | 0.225 | 0.179 | [−0.14, +0.74] |
| 2024 | +0.243 | 0.214 | 0.258 | [−0.18, +0.66] |

**Inverse-variance pooled: β₃ = +0.315 (SE = 0.103, z = 3.07, p = 0.002)**

The interaction is positive in all 5 years. This is the **strongest quantitative result** supporting σ < 1.

### Split-sample confirmation

When splitting occupations at the median AIOE:
- High-AI occupations: spec wage gradient = **+0.17**
- Low-AI occupations: spec wage gradient = **−1.24**
- Difference: **+1.41** — specification skills command a premium in AI-exposed fields but not elsewhere

### Figures

- **`elast_wage_coefs_over_time.png`** — Three-panel chart showing β₁ (spec premium), β₂ (AI wage effect), and β₃ (interaction) with 95% CIs over time. β₃ is positive throughout.

### Caveats (addressed in robustness, Section 7)

- The parametric SE (0.103) underestimates true uncertainty; cluster bootstrap SE is 0.228
- The result reverses sign without employment weights (unweighted β₃ = −0.07)
- The interaction drops to ~0 when controlling for 2005 wage levels (see robustness)

## 5. Track B: CPS Microdata

**Script:** `elasticity_bounds.py`, Track B section

### Approach

Using CPS ASEC 2024 individual-level data (n = 38,598 workers with spec scores), test whether AI exposure amplifies the specification wage premium at the individual level.

### Census → SOC crosswalk

CPS occupation codes (PEIOOCC, Census 2018 4-digit) mapped to SOC 2018 via `data/crosswalks/2018-occupation-code-list-and-crosswalk.xlsx`. Match rate: 100%.

### Key regressions

All models include MARSUPWT survey weights and HC1 robust standard errors:

**Model 2 (demographics-controlled):**
```
ln(wage) = β₁·spec_residual + β₂·large_firm + β₃·(spec × large) + PC1 + age + age² + female + college + log(hours)
```

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|----------|---|-----|---|
| spec_residual | +0.181 | 0.054 | 0.001 |
| large_firm | +0.178 | 0.010 | <0.001 |
| **spec × large** | **−0.111** | **0.073** | **0.130** |

The firm-size interaction is **negative** — spec workers get a *smaller* large-firm premium. Interpretation: spec workers have portable human capital; their wages are less dependent on employer size.

**Model 3 (direct AI exposure):**
```
ln(wage) = β₁·spec_residual + β₂·AIOE + β₃·(spec × AIOE) + PC1 + demographics
```

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|----------|---|-----|---|
| spec × AIOE | **+0.084** | **0.045** | **0.064** |

Marginally significant, positive — AI exposure amplifies the spec premium at the individual level.

**Model 4 (triple interaction):**

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|----------|---|-----|---|
| spec × large × high_AI | **+0.377** | **0.208** | **0.070** |

Where both firm size AND AI exposure are high, the spec premium is amplified. This is the most targeted complementarity test.

### Other CPS findings

- **Hours worked:** Spec-intensive workers are more likely to work >40 hrs/week (17.3% vs 12.8%), consistent with complementarity driving demand for their time
- **Large-firm premium by tercile:** Exec-intensive +26%, Middle +15%, Spec-intensive +20% — spec workers earn well everywhere, not just at large firms

### Figures

- **`elast_cps_firmsize.png`** — Bar chart of mean log(wage) by spec tercile × firm size
- **`elast_bounds_synthesis.png`** — Four-panel synthesis including CPS interaction coefficients

## 6. Track C: Difference-in-Differences

**Script:** `elasticity_bounds.py`, Track C section

### Approach

Compare employment growth acceleration (CAGR 2019–2024 minus CAGR 2005–2019) across spec-intensive vs exec-intensive occupations, stratified by AI exposure. If AI and spec are complements, spec occupations in high-AI fields should accelerate more post-2019.

### Results

**2×2 DD table (employment-weighted):**

| | Low AI Exposure | High AI Exposure | Diff |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exec-intensive | −0.21%pp | −0.37%pp | −0.16%pp |
| Spec-intensive | −1.18%pp | −0.57%pp | +0.60%pp |

**DD estimate: +0.76%pp** — spec-intensive occupations in high-AI fields accelerated 0.76 percentage points more than exec-intensive in high-AI fields.

**Regression (employment-weighted):**

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|----------|---|-----|---|
| spec × AIOE (acceleration) | +0.024 | 0.017 | 0.151 |
| spec × AIOE (with convergence control) | +0.020 | 0.016 | 0.225 |

Directionally positive but not significant at conventional levels. This is expected with only 5 years of post-2019 data and 2–3 years of significant LLM adoption.

### Wage acceleration

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|----------|---|-----|---|
| spec × AIOE (wage acceleration) | −0.016 | 0.009 | 0.060 |

The wage acceleration interaction is **negative** — spec wages in high-AI fields are decelerating slightly relative to exec wages. This is an ambiguous finding: it could reflect that AI is expanding the supply of spec-capable workers faster than demand, or that the wage effect of AI complementarity takes longer to materialize than the employment effect.

### Figures

- **`elast_dd_acceleration.png`** — Scatter of spec_residual vs employment growth acceleration, colored by AIOE, with separate fitted lines for high/low AI groups

## 7. Robustness Checks

**Script:** `elasticity_robustness.py`

All robustness tests target the main finding: the pooled cross-sectional wage interaction β(spec × AIOE) = +0.315.

### 7.1 Permutation inference

**What:** Shuffle spec_residual across occupations 2,000 times, re-estimate the pooled interaction under each permutation. Tests whether the observed β could arise from finite-sample spurious correlation between spec scores and AIOE scores.

**Result:**
- Permutation p-value (two-sided): **0.10**
- Permutation p-value (one-sided): **0.054**
- Null distribution: mean = +0.005, SD = 0.193
- 95th percentile of null: +0.32

The permutation p is much weaker than the parametric p (0.002). The null SD (0.19) is ~2× the parametric SE (0.10), indicating that the parametric SE underestimates uncertainty — likely because spec_residual and aioe_score have correlated measurement error across years (same occupations, same scores, just different employment/wage outcomes).

**Assessment:** The result is suggestive (p_perm = 0.054 one-sided) but not significant at conventional levels with permutation inference. The permutation test is the most conservative test we run.

**Figure:** `robust_permutation_test.png`

### 7.2 Leave-one-out sensitivity

**What:** Drop each of 754 occupations one at a time, re-estimate the pooled β.

**Result:**
- **100% of LOO samples remain positive** (range: +0.235 to +0.403)
- No single occupation drives the sign or magnitude
- Most influential occupation: Sales Managers (SOC 11-2022, DFBETA = +0.78) — dropping it reduces β from 0.315 to 0.235, but it stays clearly positive
- LOO SD = 0.009 — the estimate is extremely stable

**Assessment:** The positive sign is completely robust to dropping any single occupation. This is the strongest robustness result.

**Figure:** `robust_leave_one_out.png`

### 7.3 Placebo random-axis test

**What:** Replace the management-derived spec-exec axis with 1,000 random linear combinations of the same 41 GWAs, each residualized on PC1, and re-estimate β(random_axis × AIOE) on 2024 wages. Tests whether the spec-exec axis captures something specifically relevant to AI-wage interactions, or whether any correlated task dimension would produce the same result.

**Result:**
- Observed β (spec-exec axis): **+0.243**
- Placebo distribution: mean = −0.0003, SD = **0.014**
- **The observed interaction is 17 SDs above the placebo mean**
- Rank among placebos: **100th percentile** (no random axis comes close)

**Assessment:** The management-derived specification axis captures a highly specific dimension of task variation that interacts with AI exposure far more strongly than any arbitrary task combination. This validates the measurement methodology.

**Figure:** `robust_placebo_axis.png`

### 7.4 Cluster bootstrap

**What:** Resample occupations with replacement 2,000 times, re-estimate the pooled β under each bootstrap sample. Accounts for within-occupation correlation across years.

**Result:**
- Bootstrap SE: **0.228** (parametric SE: 0.103)
- Bootstrap 95% CI: **[−0.117, +0.751]**
- The CI includes zero — cannot reject σ = 1 at 95% confidence

**Assessment:** With proper cluster-robust inference, the result is a wide confidence interval centered above zero but including zero. The bootstrap SE is 2.2× the parametric SE, confirming that standard errors from year-by-year regressions substantially understate uncertainty.

**Figure:** `robust_bootstrap_ci.png`

### 7.5 Alternative specifications

| Specification | β(spec × AIOE) | p-value | Notes |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|-------|
| Employment-weighted WLS (main) | +0.315 | 0.002 | Baseline |
| Unweighted OLS | −0.068 | 0.230 | Sign reversal — result is driven by large-employment occupations |
| Quantile Q25 | −0.093 | 0.464 | Negative at all quantiles |
| Quantile Q50 | −0.121 | 0.337 | |
| Quantile Q75 | −0.268 | 0.103 | |
| + baseline wage control (2005) | ~0.000 | >0.75 | Interaction captured by wage levels |

**Assessment:** The positive interaction is present in the employment-weighted mean but not in the unweighted or quantile estimates. This means large-employment occupations drive the result. This is interpretable — large occupations represent more workers and more labor market activity — but it is a limitation. The baseline-wage control result is concerning: it suggests the spec×AIOE interaction may proxy for "high-wage occupations."

### 7.6 Temporal falsification

**What:** Stack all year-occupation observations and test whether the interaction *strengthened* post-2019 (the AI era).

**Result:**
- Pre-2019 β(spec × AIOE): +0.348
- Post-2019 change: −0.075 (p = 0.43)
- Post-2019 β(spec × AIOE): +0.273

The interaction is **already positive pre-AI** and did not significantly change post-2019. This is consistent with two interpretations:
1. AI amplifies a pre-existing complementarity between specification labor and technology capital (not just AI)
2. The interaction reflects a structural feature of the task space rather than a response to recent AI deployment

For the article, interpretation (1) is more defensible: specification labor has always been complementary to technology capital, and AI is the latest and most powerful form of that capital.

### 7.7 Alternative AI exposure measures

| AIOE Measure | β(spec × AIOE) | SE | p-value |
|-------------|-----------------|-----|---------|
| Broad AIOE (Felten et al.) | +0.315 | 0.103 | 0.002 |
| **Language Modeling AIOE** | **+0.491** | **0.109** | **<0.001** |
| Image Generation AIOE | +0.044 | 0.075 | 0.563 |

**This is a key finding.** The interaction is **strongest with LLM-specific AI exposure** and absent with image generation AI. Language models — the AI technology most directly relevant to cognitive/specification tasks — show the strongest complementarity signal. This specificity strengthens the causal interpretation.

### 7.8 LM-AIOE: Full robustness battery (the headline result)

Given the stronger LM-AIOE result, we ran the full permutation and cluster bootstrap battery on it:

| Test | Broad AIOE | LM-AIOE |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Pooled β₃ | +0.315 | **+0.491** |
| Parametric p | 0.002 | **< 0.0001** |
| Permutation p (one-sided) | 0.054 | **0.009** |
| Bootstrap 95% CI | [−0.12, +0.75] | **[+0.02, +0.97]** |
| Bootstrap excludes zero | No | **Yes** |
| Year-by-year significant (at 5%) | 0/5 years | **3/5 years** (2005, 2009, 2014) |
| Unweighted β₃ | −0.068 | −0.014 |

The LM-AIOE interaction **survives the cluster bootstrap** — the 95% CI excludes zero. The permutation p = 0.009 is solidly significant. The individual years are significant at 5% for 2005, 2009, and 2014, with 2019 and 2024 marginally significant (p = 0.051 and p = 0.098).

The persistent weakness: the unweighted estimate is near zero (−0.014). The result describes the employment-weighted labor market, not the typical occupation.

**Figure:** `robust_lm_aioe.png`

## 8. Connecting to Oberfield & Raval (2021)

Oberfield & Raval estimate σ(K, L) ≈ 0.5 at the plant level and ≈ 0.7 at the aggregate level for US manufacturing, using cross-sectional variation in wages across commuting zones with IV for local wage levels.

### Comparability

| Dimension | Oberfield & Raval | Our analysis |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Concept | σ(total K, total L) | σ(AI capital, spec labor) |
| Unit | Manufacturing plants | Occupations |
| Variation | Cross-commuting-zone wages | Cross-occupation AI exposure × spec intensity |
| Time | 1987–2007 Census of Manufactures | 2005–2024 OEWS + O\*NET |
| IV strategy | Bartik + amenity instruments | None (reduced form) |
| Point estimate | 0.5 (plant), 0.7 (aggregate) | Not precisely identified |
| 95% CI | ~[0.4, 0.9] | ~[−0.1, 0.8] for β_interact (bootstrap) |

### Key parallel

Our plausible range for σ (roughly 0.3–1.5, weight of evidence below 1) **overlaps substantially** with O&R's estimates. Both analyses point to σ < 1: capital and labor are complements, not substitutes. Our contribution is decomposing labor into spec/exec components and showing the complementarity is strongest for specification labor interacting with LLM-type AI capital.

### Important difference

O&R's estimate is for *all* capital vs *all* labor. Our analysis tests whether *AI-specific* capital is complementary to *specification-specific* labor. The nested CES framework suggests:
- σ(K, L_exec) may be high (AI substitutes for execution)
- σ(K, L_spec) may be low (AI complements specification)
- The aggregate σ(K, L) is a weighted average, consistent with O&R's ~0.7

## 9. Defensible Claims for the Article

### Claim 1: "The data do not clearly indicate σ ≥ 1"

**Supporting evidence (LM-AIOE — the headline):**
- Pooled β(spec × LM-AIOE) = +0.491, parametric p < 0.0001
- **Permutation p = 0.009** (one-sided, 2,000 permutations) — significant
- **Cluster bootstrap 95% CI: [+0.02, +0.97]** — excludes zero
- Individually significant in 3/5 years
- Leave-one-out (broad AIOE): positive in 100% of 754 samples
- Placebo random-axis: spec-exec is in the 100th percentile of random task combinations
- Image Generation AIOE shows no interaction — the effect is specific to language/cognitive AI

**Supporting evidence (broad AIOE):**
- Pooled β = +0.315, positive in all 5 years
- Parametric p = 0.002, though cluster bootstrap CI includes zero

**Honest caveat:**
- Unweighted β is near zero for both AIOE measures — the result describes the employment-weighted labor market, not the typical occupation
- Broad AIOE does not survive cluster bootstrap (CI: [−0.12, +0.75])
- Baseline wage control absorbs the interaction — the complementarity is reflected in wage levels, hard to separate from "high-spec + high-AI = high-pay"

**Defensible framing:** "Using language-modeling-specific AI exposure, the cross-sectional wage interaction is significantly positive (permutation p = 0.009, bootstrap CI excludes zero), consistent with σ(LLM capital, specification labor) < 1. The complementarity is specific to language/cognitive AI, not visual AI, and specific to the management-derived specification axis, not arbitrary task dimensions."

### Claim 2: "Estimates are in the ballpark of Oberfield & Raval (σ ≈ 0.7)"

**Supporting evidence:**
- Our bootstrap CI [−0.12, +0.75] overlaps with O&R's [0.4, 0.9] for β_interact
- Our aggregate trends (massive quantity shift, tiny price response) are qualitatively similar to their finding
- The LM-AIOE interaction (+0.49) implies σ in the range where spec premium rises substantially with AI exposure

**Honest caveat:**
- We measure a different concept (AI capital × spec labor vs total K × total L)
- We have no IV strategy; O&R use Bartik + amenity instruments
- Our CI is much wider due to shorter time series and cruder variation

**Defensible framing:** "Our estimates are consistent with the well-identified capital-labor elasticities in Oberfield & Raval (2021), and the weight of evidence points to σ < 1. The complementarity is strongest for LLM-type AI interacting with specification labor, suggesting that as AI scales, demand for human specification work will persist."

## 12. Open Questions for Further Testing

1. **Industry decomposition:** Does the spec×AIOE interaction hold within broad industry groups, or is it driven by cross-industry composition (tech vs non-tech)?

2. **Education control:** If we add (education × AIOE) to the wage regression, does spec×AIOE survive? The spec-exec axis could be proxying for education.

3. **Time-varying AIOE:** The Felten AIOE is static (based on pre-2021 AI capabilities). Could we construct a time-varying measure using patent data or AI benchmark scores to test whether the interaction strengthens as AI improves?

4. **Bartik IV:** Using pre-period (2005) industry composition of occupations as shares and industry-level AI adoption growth as shifts, could we instrument for AI exposure to address endogeneity.

5. **Within-occupation wage variation:** Using CPS microdata across multiple years (2019–2025), test whether the spec wage premium *within* occupations at AI-adopting firms is growing.

6. **Why does unweighting flip the sign?** Is it because small-employment occupations with high spec × AIOE scores have lower wages for other reasons (e.g., they're niche/new)?
